I'm sitting here watching the (Buddy Guy and Miles Caton Tiny Desk Concert)[https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m5XxOLdMSS8], thinking about this year's Black History Month. I marvel at Buddy Guy's hands as he pulls magic out his electric guitar. They remind me of my grandmother's hands, bent with age but still skilled at movements they've repeated for decades. {% include zb_bloc.html %} Whether picking guitar strings or mixing ingredients for perfect crispy-edged pancakes, these hands tell tales. I smile at the gentle encouragement Guy gives Caton throughout the segment. Caton is clearly honored to share this space with a legend, not quite sure how to take up space without overstepping any of the unwritten rules one must follow when sharing the stage with an expert bluesman. I think about centuries of circumstances that made this Tiny Desk Concert possible, from the (Transatlantic Slave Trade)[https://eji.org/report/transatlantic-slave-trade/origins/#the-european-influence-on-africa] to the development of (gospel music)[https://www.loc.gov/collections/songs-of-america/articles-and-essays/musical-styles/ritual-and-worship/african-american-gospel] and (blues music)[https://www.aaihs.org/the-historical-roots-of-blues-music/]. 


It is the intergenerational bond, the transfer of energy and knowledge between Guy and Caton that makes this particular Tiny Desk special to me. As a community, we need more of this bittersweet collaboration between old and young. As a fan of blues music myself, I hope that more young singers and musicians like Caton embrace the genre. In the meantime, peace and blessings to the elders willing to mentor the young.
